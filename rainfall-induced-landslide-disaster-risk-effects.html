Rainfall-induced landslides are a common occurrence in terrain with steep topography and soils that have degradable strength. Answer:

Modifying slope geometry, using chemical agents to reinforce slope material, constructing structures such as piling and retaining walls, grouting rock seams and fissures, diverting waste routes, and rerouting surface and underwater drainage are all direct techniques of preventing landslides.

28. similarities and difference of rainfall induced landslide and sinkhole


Answer: Similarities: signs and triggering factors

difference: the soil's behavior

Explanation:

The similarities between rainfall induced landslide and sinkholes is their signs; the collapsing of the soil often starts from sliding away from its foundation, as well as the tilts or the cracks that can be seen on a concretes.

The triggering factors are also similar. According to ( https://www.state.nj.us/njoem/mitigation/pdf/2019/mit2019_section5-7_Geo_Hazards.pdf ) both landslide and sinkholes can occur due to 1) erosion by rivers, glaciers, or ocean waves which create over- steepened slopes; (2) rock and soil slopes weakened through saturation by snowmelt or heavy rains; (3) earthquakes which create stresses making weak slopes fail; and (4) excess weight from rain/snow accumulation, rock/ore stockpiling, waste piles, or man-made structures

However, the difference is that a sinkhole forms when a cavern collapses, while the landslide occurs when material on a steep slope gives way, either from rainfall or an earthquake.

29. what are the factors that contribute to a province's high risk to earthquake induced shallow landslide? ​


Answer:

An earthquake is the ground shaking caused by a sudden slip on a fault. Stresses in the earth's outer layer push the sides of the fault together. Stress builds up and the rocks slip suddenly, releasing energy in waves that travel through the earth's crust and cause the shaking that we feel during an earthquake.

Faults are caused by the tectonic plates grinding and scraping against each other as they continuously and slowly move. In California, for example, there are two plates - the Pacific Plate (which extends from western California to Japan, including much of the Pacific Ocean floor) and the North American Plate (which is most of the North American continent and parts of the Atlantic Ocean). The Pacific Plate moves northwestward past the North American Plate along the San Andreas Fault at a rate of about two inches per year.
