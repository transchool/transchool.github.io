Answer:

There are several differences that can be observed between a specimen that is stained and a specimen that is not when viewed under a microscope.

One of the main differences is that a stained specimen will generally have more contrast and be easier to see against the background of the microscope slide. This is because the stain adds color to the specimen, which makes it stand out more clearly.

Another difference is that a stained specimen may reveal more details or structures within the specimen that may not be visible in an unstained specimen. This is because different stains can be used to highlight specific structures or components within the specimen, making them more visible under the microscope.

The purpose of staining a specimen before viewing it under the microscope is to make it easier to see and study. Stains can help to improve the contrast and clarity of the specimen, making it easier to see and identify specific structures or features. Additionally, different stains can be used to highlight specific components or structures within the specimen, allowing researchers to study these features more closely.
