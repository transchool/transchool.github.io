A 14 Step Guide To Successfully Engage Existing Communities. Compared to conventional marketing strategies which consist of sending out sales messages via mass media campaigns,  there are many benefits to interacting with established audiences, hoping that some people will pick them up.  Community participation is no longer about company  or profits, it's about enthusiasm and authenticity.  Achievement is tricky, but incredibly rewarding because it will  allow you to develop long-lasting relationships with potential clients.  

Here's how I  am doing it.  Be sure to let me know  if you disagree or take another approach.

1.  As with defining target demographics in traditional marketing, the first step is  to identify established communities which share the values and passion of your brand.  

2.  You have to become a member of their community before members  

of an established community can become a part of your brand community.  You as a individual, and  not as a brand representative.

3.  Serve  supportive.  Answer to questions from  users, share your expertise.  

4.  Identify and get to  know the community's key people.  Understand their beliefs, and what pushes  them inside the group to be involved.  

5.  Identify what is  missing from the Group.  What sort of  frustrations are common?  Anything should be done to  get the society going forward?

6.  Identify the same items for  key individuals within the group.  What are their aspirations, things inside  the culture that they want to achieve?  Who holds them  out of this?  

7.  When you understand what a group is missing, and its  people, determine what you or your company can do to help.

8.  Discuss with the group these frustrations / problems  / ideas /opportunities,  and offer your assistance.  Or throw yourself in ideas, mention your brand, and see how  people react to that idea and how it could affect your company.  

9.  Identify participants from these discussions who are inspired  to take part in, or even lead, the project.

10.  Since day 1 on the project make sure  that every move is recorded somewhere in public.  Whether on the website of the initiative, the website of  the group or the website (or other channels of your brand).  A Group member will tell  the story of the project.  If that is not feasible, tell the story yourself-as  a member of the group, not as a company employee.

11.  Make sure anyone who participates  actively in the project gets attention.  Allow them proud to be part of this or  other ventures, and allow other group members want to participate.  Or even come up with  ideas for your own project.

12.  Keep in mind that when you interact you do not define the project as an  

activity of your brand, but as a collective effort to which you and your company belong.  (Example: ' 20 percent time ' from Google)

13.  Throughout each group, bear in mind: Every  member of the community is a potential collaborator.  If not on this one, maybe it's  the next or the next one after that.  To become a delegate or even an initiative-taker, it  

takes time to ' trigger ' a group member.

14. If a key player is absent from the community to do a  

particular role on a project: don't introduce yourself anyone outside the community.  That'll feel  pretty invasive.  Seek to find somebody in the neighborhood, or ask  if they know anyone who can do the job.
