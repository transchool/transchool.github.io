14. 32. An ecologically healthy area has____. A. A few species with a sustainable number of individuals of each species B. Many species with a sustainable number of individuals of each species C. A few species with very few individuals of each species D. Many species with very few individuals of each species


Based on the options given, an ecologically healthy area has: D. Many species with very few individuals of each species. Some characteristics of healthy ecology:

1. Many species with very few individuals of each species

2. The quality of the air is clean, fresh and cool

3. Waste management

4. Smooth drains and no smell

5. Lots of green plants

EXPLANATION:

1. Many species with very few individuals of each species

Many species but very few individuals of each species. This is the ideal comparison. Because humans can usually interfere with the number or quality of species in the environment

2. The quality of the air is clean, fresh and cool

A healthy environment has clean, fresh and cool air quality. The air is neither black nor white, nor does it smell.

3. Waste management

Not only having a landfill, there should also be further management. For example, organic and inorganic waste management. If not managed, waste will accumulate and eventually make the environment dirty and unhealthy.

4. Smooth drains and no smell

Another feature of a healthy environment is smooth and odorless drains. Smooth water channels enable the community to carry out BWT (bathing, washing, and toilet) activities properly. So that the health of the environment and the population is maintained.

5. Lots of green plants

In order to be called a healthy environment, the environment must be overgrown with green plants. So that it is not arid and the surrounding air is kept clean. The existence of good waste management A healthy environment also has the characteristics of good waste management in every house. This management is intended to prevent environmental pollution, and maintain cleanliness.

The answer is letter B.

Reproduction (or procreation) is the biological mechanism by which new offspring (individual organisms) are created by their parents. "This is an essential characteristic of all human life that every particular organism lives as a result of reproduction. Most notably, reproduction is crucial for the future of the species. Established reproductive strategies are typically classified into two major types: sexual and asexual. In the case of asexual reproduction, an individual can replicate without the intervention of another individual of that species. An example of asexual reproduction is the division of a bacterial cell into two daughter cells. This method of reproduction creates genetically identical species (clones), whereas in sexual reproduction the genetic material of two individuals is mixed to create children that are genetically distinct from their parents. During sexual reproduction, the male gamete (sperm) may be inserted within the female body for internal fertilization or the sperm and eggs may be released into the atmosphere for external fertilization. Humans are an example of the former, while seahorses are an example of the latter. Following a mating dance, the female seahorse lay eggs in the male seahorse's abdominal brood pouch where they are fertilized. Egg hatch and offspring have been developing in the pouch for several weeks. Sexual reproduction means that the gene pool of the species is mixed. Variations in the offspring of sexual reproduction make it possible for certain individuals to survive and provide a mechanism for selective adaptation. In addition, sexual reproduction typically results in the development of a life stage that is capable of sustaining the pressures that endanger the offspring of an asexual parent. Thus, seeds, spores, larvae, pupae, cysts, or other "overwintering" periods of sexual reproduction guarantee longevity during unfavorable conditions when the individual can "wait out" undesirable circumstances before a return to suitability occurs. Sexual reproduction is a combination of (usually haploid or possessing a single set of unpaired chromosomes) reproductive cells from two individuals to form a single offspring (usually diploid or having a pair of each kind of chromosome). Sexual reproduction produces offspring with novel gene variations. This can be an adaptation advantage in chaotic or volatile conditions. As humans, we are used to speaking of animals as having two distinct sexes, male and female, defined at conception. However, there are also variants on this issue in the animal world.

You first need to calculate the total area of your forest and the area covered by each species. This may be available from inventory data or you might need to carry out a forest survey.

If you have a list of smaller forest components you need to calculate the total area for each species. You can do this in Microsoft Excel or any statistical or GIS software. A worked step by step method using Excel is available.

Species Richness

Species richness is the number of species present in the forest. For small datasets it can be calculated by counting the number of species in your forest manually. For larger datasets we provide a worked example.

Species Evenness

Species evenness describes the relative abundance of each species. Once you have measured the area of each species in your forest you can see how evenly they are distributed. The percentage area of the most dominant species can be helpful in understanding evenness. A worked example is available.

The Shannon Index

The Shannon Index combines species richness and evenness into a single indicator. You can calculate the Shannon Index in Microsoft Excel or other statistical software
