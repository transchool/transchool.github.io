When an equation is written in its general form, such as 3x + 6y = 12, finding the intercepts becomes easier.

1. y = 2x + 2 and 2y = 4x - 4

Solution:

Step 1 - Choose three x  values and list them in a table to graph the equation. Choose the values that are simple and simple to calculate.

For y = 2x + 2

x = 0, y = 2(0) + 2 = 2 → (x, y) = (0, 2) x = 1, y = 2(1) + 2 → (x, y) = (1, 4) x = -1, y = 2(-1) + 2 → (x, y) = (-1, 0)

For 2y = 4x - 4 ⇔ y = 2x - 2

x = 0, y = 2(0) - 2 = -2 → (x, y) = (0, -2) x = 1, y = 2(1) - 2 → (x, y) = (1, 0) x = -1, y = 2(-1) - 2 → (x, y) = (-1, -4)

Step 2 - Draw a graph of the ordered pairs discovered for y = 2x + 2 and 2y = 4x - 4  in step 1.

Step 3 - Draw a straight line through the points you've marked.

Linear equations are of the form [tex]Ax+By=C[/tex] where [tex]A, B, C, \in \mathbb{Z}[/tex]. Linear equations have also a degree (highest exponent) of 1.

Different Types of Writing Linear EquationsStandard FormSlope-intercept FormPoint-Slope FormIntercept Form

There are different ways to graph a linear equation.

Steps in Graphing  a Linear Equation on the Coordinate PlaneChoose at least two independent values for [tex]x[/tex].Make sure that the chosen values are a few units apart from each other. Substitute the values of [tex]x[/tex] into the equation and solve for the value of [tex]y[/tex].Form the ordered pairs and plot these two points on the plane. Connect these two points and draw an arrowhead at both ends of the line.

Solutions

1. [tex]y=2x+2[/tex] and [tex]2y=4x+4[/tex]

          [tex]\begin{matrix}x & y=2x+2 & (x,y)\\\\0 & y=2(0)+2=2 & (0, 2)\\5 & y=2(5)+2=12 & (5, 12)\end{matrix}[/tex]          

          [tex]\begin{matrix}x & 2y=4x+4 & (x,y)\\\\0 & 2y=4(0)+4&(0,2)\\& 2y=4\\&y=2\\5 & 2y=4(5)+4&(5, 12)\\&2y=24\\&y=12\end{matrix}[/tex]

       The equations in number 1 are equivalent equations thus these two equations lie on the same line.

2. [tex]y=x-5[/tex] and [tex]y=-x+4[/tex]

         [tex]\begin{matrix}x & y=x-5 & (x,y)\\\\0 & y=0-5 =-5& (0, -5)\\5 & y=5-5=0 & (5, 0)\end{matrix}[/tex]

         [tex]\begin{matrix}x & y=-x+4 & (x,y)\\\\0 & y=0+4=4& (0, 4)\\5 & y=-5+4=-1 & (5, -1)\end{matrix}[/tex]

3. [tex]y=2x-3[/tex] and [tex]y=2x+4[/tex]

         [tex]\begin{matrix}x & y=2x-3 & (x,y)\\\\0 & y=2(0)-3=-3& (0, -3)\\5 & y=2(5)-3=7 & (5, 7)\end{matrix}[/tex]

          [tex]\begin{matrix}x & y=2x+4 & (x,y)\\\\0 & y=2(0)+4 =4& (0, 4)\\5 & y=2(5)+4=14 & (5, 14)\end{matrix}[/tex]
