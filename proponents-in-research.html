Title: Exploring the Experience of Science Teachers in Incorporating Technology in Their Teaching Practice

Proponents/Researchers names: Deborah A. Crockett, Todd Campbell

Abstract: This qualitative study explores the experiences of secondary science teachers in incorporating technology into their teaching practice. Eight science teachers were interviewed to understand how they use technology in their teaching, their perceptions of the benefits and challenges of using technology in their classrooms, and the impact of technology integration on student learning. Data analysis revealed that teachers experienced a range of benefits from technology integration, including increased student engagement and motivation, improved communication and collaboration, and enhanced opportunities for differentiation. However, they also faced a number of challenges, including a lack of training and support, difficulties in selecting appropriate technologies, and concerns about equity and access. Implications for practice and future research are discussed.

Title: Understanding Undergraduate Engineering Students’ Perceptions of Teamwork

Proponents/Researchers names: Dr. Kevin Grosskopf, Dr. David Knight

Abstract: This qualitative study explores undergraduate engineering students' perceptions of teamwork in project-based courses. Data were collected through semi-structured interviews with 12 students enrolled in two different engineering courses. Analysis of the interviews revealed several themes related to students' perceptions of teamwork, including the importance of communication, leadership, and conflict resolution, the challenges of working with diverse team members, and the impact of teamwork on learning and motivation. The findings suggest that effective teamwork requires more than just technical skills and that engineering educators need to explicitly teach and assess teamwork skills to prepare students for the demands of the workforce.

Title: A Qualitative Study of Students’ Attitudes towards Computer Science

Proponents/Researchers names: Jennie Murack, Beth Simon, Leo Porter

Abstract: This paper presents a qualitative study of students’ attitudes towards computer science. Data were collected through semi-structured interviews with 21 students enrolled in an introductory computer science course. Analysis of the interviews revealed several themes related to students' attitudes towards computer science, including their perceptions of the field, their experiences in the course, and the impact of prior programming experience on their attitudes. The findings suggest that students' attitudes towards computer science are influenced by a variety of factors, including gender, prior programming experience, and perceptions of the field. Implications for practice and future research are discussed.

Title: Teachers’ Perceptions of Using Inquiry-Based Learning in Mathematics Classrooms

Proponents/Researchers names: Carla C. Johnson, Faith E. Arezzo

Abstract: This qualitative study explores the perceptions of secondary mathematics teachers in using inquiry-based learning (IBL) in their classrooms. Six mathematics teachers were interviewed to understand how they implement IBL, their perceptions of the benefits and challenges of using IBL in their classrooms, and the impact of IBL on student learning. Data analysis revealed that teachers experienced a range of benefits from IBL, including increased student engagement and motivation, improved problem-solving skills, and enhanced opportunities for differentiation. However, they also faced a number of challenges, including a lack of resources and support, difficulties in adapting to a new teaching style, and concerns about student preparation. Implications for practice and future research are discussed.

30. proponents of literature


The law and literature movement focuses on the interdisciplinary connection between lawand literature. This field has roots in two major developments in the intellectual history of law—first, the growing doubt about whether law in isolation is a source of value and meaning, or whether it must be plugged into a large cultural or philosophical or social-science context to give it value and meaning; and, second, the growing focus on the mutability of meaning in all texts, whether literary or legal. Those who work in the field stress one or the other of two complementary perspectives: Law in literature (understanding enduring issues as they are explored in great literary texts) and law as literature(understanding legal texts by reference to methods of literary interpretation, analysis, and critique).

This movement has broad and potentially far reaching implications with regards to future teaching methods, scholarship, and interpretations of legal texts. Combining literature's ability to provide unique insight into the human condition through text with the legal framework that regulates those human experiences in reality gives a democratic judiciary a new and dynamic approach to reaching the aims of providing a just and moral society. It is necessary, in practical thought and discussion about the use of legal rhetoric, to understand text's role in defining human experience.
