12. Philippines facing unemployment​


Answer:

Unemployment rate is at 5.1%

Explanation:

The Philippines has an unemployment rate of 5.1% which means that those who are employed are at 94.9% based on recent statistics. The rate is relatively low compared to other countries. Other nations have as high as 50% rate (in war-torn Syria) while others go as low as .3% in Cambodia.

What needs to be looked at, however, is the underemployment rate. These are those who are working but are in jobs not in line with what they studied in college. This also means that they are working below what they are capable of. The underemployment rate currently stands at 13.5% which is comparatively high.

There is, in contrast, bad underemployment and good underemployment. Bad underemployment happens when the person is underemployed because he or she has no choice, whether because there is no open job positions or he was recently retrenched from a previous post. Good underemployment, on the other hand, is when an engineer for example, decides to become a flower shop owner because that is his passion and it was out of his own free will.

As for unemployment, this can be solved by preparing potential workers for what is available in the market. There are many job openings but there are only a few who are qualified to take on the job. The job cannot adjust, it should be the applicant. If skills are lacking then skill-building must be done and this is something that the government can work on.

22. actions on how to solve unemployment during covid-19​


Answer:

The Covid-19 crisis has profoundly affected employment everywhere. The evidence is now available on the impacts seen during the first few months of the pandemic (ILO 2020). In some countries, like the US and Canada, unemployment quickly skyrocketed to levels not seen in living memory. In others, including several in Europe, the labour market response looked very different, with few layoffs and workers remaining attached to their employer (even if they were working little, or not at all). Various factors explain these differences in how labour markets adjusted. For example, the severity of the pandemic, the structure of the economy, and the implicit social contract between employers and employees all played an important part in determining the effect of the pandemic on labour markets.
