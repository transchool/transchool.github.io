24. 5 examples of non decaying materials and decaying materials


5 examples of non-decaying materials and decaying materials.

Here are 5 examples of materials that may be considered as non-decaying:

Aluminum. Polystyrene or styrofoam. Glass. Gold. Most types of plastics.

Non-decaying materials do not go through the natural process of bacterial degradation. They can be reduced into another form, but only through human effort. Many of these materials such as gold, glass, aluminum, and others serve certain industrial purposes. In recent decades, the production and use of materials such as plastics and polystyrene have gone out of control due to its low manufacturing cost and convenience. The disposable society we have become creates a dangerous and unsightly view in many places.

Here are 5 examples of materials that do decay:

Wood. Paper. Living things (plants and animals). Regular steel. Rocks.  

Natural decay happens through the environmental process and bacterial action. We all know that wood degrades when left outside, it will be fed on by termites and other small creatures. What happens to paper after some time is obvious, it crumbles into dust after years of staying dry and disintegrates when it is subjected to rain. Living things are broken down by critters and bacteria after it has passed away. Regular steel goes through the process of oxidation which turns it into iron oxide (rust). Although the entry of rocks at number five seems to be a mistake, they do decay on a regular basis, this is the reason we have clay deposits around the world. Certain bacteria dissolve rocks by secreting acid as they feed on it. It is not obvious but it does happen all the time.

Decay Materials and Don't Undergo Decay Materials

Decomposition is a chemical change that causes the object to be damaged by the composition/structure carried out by the decomposer or decay medium, resulting in physical changes to the decaying material.

While materials that do not undergo decay are materials that are anti-microorganisms, so they cannot decompose or decompose. Materials that are long enough to decompose generally contain little water, thus inhibiting the growth of microorganisms in these materials (materials derived from living things).

Meanwhile, materials that are inanimate objects tend to be non-decomposing because they cannot be decomposed by microorganisms.

The materials that can decompose and cannot decompose are as follows:

Decomposable materialFruitRiceOnionChilliVegetables

The above materials are materials derived from living organisms, namely plants. This material contains water in it. Decay in these materials will occur in a certain time depending on the water content. If the storage of these materials is not appropriate, then spoilage will occur more quickly. One way to slow down the spoilage of these foodstuffs is by storing them in the right places.

Materials that do not undergo decay:

PlateForkWokSpatulaPure of Water

For the above materials are equipment that is often found in the kitchen and is not subject to decay. The equipment is included in inanimate objects that cannot absorb water, so that water can only be found on the surface of objects that can be lost or dry due to wind or hot temperatures. However, for pure or unmixed water, it does not decay.

Other ingredients that can be found in the kitchen where the water content is little or no, such as salt, dry spices, green beans, and oil can undergo spoilage but will occur over a long period of time. This is due to the low moisture content of these materials and proper storage treatment.
