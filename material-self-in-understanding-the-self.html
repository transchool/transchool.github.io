2. what is material self in understanding the self​


Answer:

The material self, according to William James, pertains to the objects, places, or even people which have the label “mine”. Such possessions are viewed as extensions of individuals' identities. For instance, your clothes reflect certain aspects of your personality and you designate them as “my wardrobe”.

6. Reading materials like "Crossing the Bar" portray particular themes and meanings.By understanding their elements, we can say that they create significant connectionsto our personal experiences and to the world we live inTEXT-TO-SELF TEXT-TO-TEXTTEXT-TO-WORLDNeed ko pasagutan namanNg maayos​


Answer:

Making connections to the text is just one way we can help learners understand what they are reading.

As readers connect the text to self, to other texts, and to events that have or are happening in the world, they have a clearer focus for using many of the other comprehension reading strategies.

There are three kinds of connections readers make before, during, or after reading:

1. text to self, which could sounds like:

This reminds me of my own life…

I can relate to this character because…

If it was me, I would…

2.  text to text, which could sounds like:

This reminds me of another book I’ve read/movie I’ve watched…

This is different from the other book because…

This is the same as the other book because…

3. text to world, which could sounds like:

This reminds me of _______ in history…

This reminds me of ________ going on in the word right now…

This is similar to _________ that’s happening in the news…

This is different to _______ that’s happening in the news…

 

Note: This part is not an activity but the discussion part to differentiate the types of connections in reading.

10. How does the interaction of your social self and material self help you understand yourself better?​


Answer:

Explanation:

We have learned so far, among other things, that people generally want to regard themselves favourably and that they have complex, well-developed self-concepts. Of course, these more mental and emotional sides of ourselves do not develop in a vacuum. They are greatly impacted by the social factors in our environment. Some of these influences have already been hinted at; for instance, in our analysis of the self-verification hypothesis, we observed how feedback from others might impact our perceptions of ourselves and our self-worth. We also considered the ways in which our sociocultural upbringing may have an impact on the elements of our self-concept.

By looking at the various ways that the social environment affects our self-concept and self-esteem, we will examine these and other social components of the self in greater detail in this part. The self is not an isolated entity; for instance, we do not perceive ourselves as shy, jazz fans, or giving people a break at birth. Instead, our observations of and interactions with others shape these views. Do you have money or not? Is it lovely or ugly? Wise or not? Do you play video games well or poorly? How do you know that? Only by looking at others around us can we find the answers to these questions. It is true to state that the social situation is the only environment in which the ego has meaning.
