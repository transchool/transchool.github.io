4. What are the five basic goal of psychology?and explain​


Answer:

Describe – The first goal is to observe behavior and describe, often in minute detail, what was observed as objectively as possible.

2. Explain –

While descriptions come from observable data, psychologists must go beyond what is obvious and explain their observations. In other words, why did the subject do what he or she did?

3. Predict –

Once we know what happens, and why it happens, we can begin to speculate what will happen in the future. There’s an old saying, which very often holds true: "the best predictor of future behavior is past behavior."

4. Control –

Once we know what happens, why it happens and what is likely to happen in the future, we can excerpt control over it. In other words, if we know you choose abusive partners because your father was abusive, we can assume you will choose another abusive partner, and can therefore intervene to change this negative behavior.

5. Improve –

Not only do psychologists attempt to control behavior, they want to do so in a positive manner, they want to improve a person’s life, not make it worse. This is not always the case, but it should always be the intention.

21. What are the concepts of psychology?


Answer:

The concepts of psychology? Going through all of them would amount to a textbook. Here are some of the basics to get you started:

Behaviour occurs in response to stimuli. The abilities to sense stimuli and respond to them are functions of the body’s nervous system. The sensation of a stimulus varies logarithmically according to its intensity.

Some behaviours, known as instincts, are inborn and are a product of evolution. Other behaviour are acquired or learned in the course of life.

Specific responses can be conditioned to occur more frequently or less frequently through association with rewards and punishments.

Individual differences in behaviour are what make up personality. The vast differences in personality boil down to just five or six major dimensions.

The behaviour of individuals often changes in social situations. Factors like conformity, culture and group dynamics have a great influence.

Behaviour is greatly facilitated by intelligence, the ability to apply one’s abilities to different problems.

Behaviour is sometimes impaired by cognitive bias, the use of inappropriate short-cuts in problem-solving. Our brains are also naturally inclined to perceive the world in terms of patterns, sometimes inappropriately.

The brain, or central nervous system, provides a huge range of mental abilities, such as coherent perception, skilful action, rational problem-solving, spatial awareness, verbal communication, general knowledge, working memory, selective attention, etc.

People with localised brain damage have helped us identify how different brain structures are involved in different mental functions. Some major functions are lateralised into either the left hemisphere or the right hemisphere.

Much of what the brain does is automatic and unconscious. Some of it, however, is conscious and self-controlled. Introspection reveals that we have a stream of consciousness. The mechanisms underlying consciousness itself remain a bit of a mystery.

A person can be strongly affected by traumatic experiences, even by a trauma that occurred years ago, in childhood. This can result in mental illness.

People are generally driven to satisfy both their natural needs and their adopted goals.

People tend to positively thrive when they experience a sense of meaning in life.
