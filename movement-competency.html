10. learn on how to move in a variety of ways alone, and with others, while applying movement competency. ​


What is movement competency?

The capacity to move well is required for participation in physical activity. Inactivity and the related health and social problems may emerge if movement skills are not taught and encouraged from a young age.

Some of the More Specialized Talents under the Heading of Movement CompetencesSending Skills - refers to how a person moves or sends something out of their reach. Throwing, kicking, headbutting, and other novel techniques are examples of this. The youngster can choose from a variety of possibilities, for instance, if the objective of a game is to strike a target. For instance, he or she could kick the ball in the direction of the target or throw it underhandedly or overhandedly.Receiving Skills - how a person catches or receives an object. This may involve catching, stopping a ball with one's foot, entangling anything in one's body, or employing other inventive techniques.Transporting Skills - moving through the environment involves a variety of techniques, such as walking, running, hopping, skipping, and jumping (upright transferring), as well as rolling and tumbling (vertical or prone transporting).Body Control Skills - balance and the ability to move one portion of our bodies while keeping the others still are two examples of body control talents. It can involve maintaining body control while moving or remaining still.Coordinated Movements - a person blends the many movement skills in play and daily activities by making coordinated motions. This can involve running while catching a ball or navigating a playground outside.

20. Most Essential Learning Competencies1. Using models or illustrations, explain how movements alongfaults generote earthquakes.2. Differentiate the epicenter of an earthquake from its focus;intensity of an earthquoke from its moanitude; and activefrom inactive faults.3. Explain how earthquake waves provideinformation about the interior of the earth.​


Answer:

1.Earthquakes are the result of sudden movement along faults within the Earth. The movement releases stored-up 'elastic strain' energy in the form of seismic waves, which propagate through the Earth and cause the ground surface to shake.

2.The focus is the place inside Earth's crust where an earthquake originates. The point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus is the epicenter. ... It's these waves that you feel during an earthquake.

*Intensity: The severity of earthquake shaking is assessed using a descriptive scale – the Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale. Magnitude: Earthquake size is a quantitative measure of the size of the earthquake at its source. The Richter Magnitude Scale measures the amount of seismic energy released by an earthquake

*Active faults are structure along which we expect displacement to occur. By definition, since a shallow earthquake is a process that produces displacement across a fault, all shallow earthquakes occur on active faults. Inactive faults are structures that we can identify, but which do no have earthquakes.

3.These waves contain vital information about the internal structure of the Earth. As seismic waves pass through the Earth, they are refracted, or bent, like rays of light bend when they pass through a glass prism. ... Both P- and S-waves from earthquakes travel through the mantle, demonstrating that it is solid.
