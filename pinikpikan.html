1. why did the child seem to be bothered by pinikpikan


Answer:

Pinikpikan comes from the Ilocano word, “pikpik”, which loosely means to hit repeatedly. Studies say that it was first cooked to appease spirits worshipped by the tribes of the Mountain Province. It was also said to have been a ritual for tribes facing a difficult decision. According to Igorot Elder and documentary photographer, Tommy Hafalla, who is based in Baguio, more often than not, the ritual is a daoes (dao-es), which means a “cleansing”.

Explanation:

In the ritual, the spirits and the ancestors are invoked to help grant what is being asked for. The divination is a loose term, referring to the act of reading the gallbladder after the chicken is dismembered. For sickness and safe travels, a full gall bladder that’s filled with thick bile is a good thing. But if one asks asking for vanquishing enemies in battle, the full gall bladder is a bad thing. “For Igorots, they summon the ancestors,” says Hafalla. While pinikpikan now aims to break all the bones in the chicken’s body to make it more tender, ritual dictates that the bones be broken and the chicken cut a certain way.
