11. Symbiotic relationship definition biology


Answer:

Symbiosis is a close ecological relationship between the individuals of two (or more) different species.   Sometimes a symbiotic relationship benefits both species, sometimes one species benefits at the other's expense, and in other cases neither species benefits.

Ecologists use a different term for each type of symbiotic relationship:

Mutualism

 --   both species benefit

Commensalism

 --   one species benefits, the other is unaffected

Parasitism

 --   one species benefits, the other is harmed

Competition

 --   neither species benefits

Neutralism

 --   both species are unaffected

27. 5 Give 3 examples of symbiotic relationships. Why do you consider it as symbiotic?​


Answer:

1. The relationship between bees and flowers is symbiotic, as the bees collect nectar from the flowers, which in turn helps to pollinate the flowers. Both the bees and the flowers benefit from this relationship.

2. The relationship between clownfish and sea anemones is symbiotic, as the clownfish live among the stinging tentacles of the sea anemone for protection from predators, while the clownfish use their fins to keep debris and parasites off of the anemones. Both the clownfish and the sea anemones benefit from this relationship.

3. The relationship between cattle and cattle egrets is symbiotic, as the cattle egrets feed on insects that bother the cattle, while the cattle stir up insects with their movement, making it easier for the egrets to find food. Both the cattle and the cattle egrets benefit from this relationship.

Explanation:

I consider these relationships as symbiotic because both species benefit from the interaction. They are mutually beneficial relationships.

A theory suggesting that the organelles such as mitochondria and chloroplasts within the eukaryotic cell came about as a result of the early endosymbiosis between prokaryotic endosymbionts and eukaryotic host cell. Synonym: symbiogenesis.

how they relate

The endosymbiotic theory states that some of the organelles in eukaryotic cells were once prokaryotic microbes. Mitochondria and chloroplasts are the same size as prokaryotic cells and divide by binary fission. Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA which is circular, not linear

evolution

The endosymbiotic theory explains how eukaryotic cells evolved. The large and small cells formed a symbiotic relationship in which both cells benefited. Some of the small cells were able to break down the large cell's wastes for energy. They supplied energy not only to themselves but also to the large cell.
