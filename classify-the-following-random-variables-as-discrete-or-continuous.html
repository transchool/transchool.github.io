27. Which of the following is not classified discrete random variable?


Answer:

What is a discrete random variable?

A discrete variable is a variable which can only take a countable number of values. Following are three examples of discrete random variables.

Example 1: I toss three coins and the variable X is the number of heads showing. So X can take on any of the values 0, 1, 2, or 3.

Example 2: The men in a certain club respond to a survey. One of the questions is “Are you, or have you ever been, a smoker?” and the allowed answers are “Currently am a smoker,” “Have smoked in the past, but don’t now,” and “Have never smoked.”

Example 3: Consider tossing a fair die. Let the variable X be the number of tosses it takes to get an outcome of 4. So X can take on any value among 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, etc. We do not know what upper limit to choose here for the number of tosses, but the possible values are countable.

To fully consider discrete variables, you will need to contrast them with the other type of variable: continuous variables. A continuous variable can take on any value in an interval.

Example 4. Consider the age of a person, in years. A person might be 12.0000 years old (on the day and exact hour and minute of their birth,) or they might be 16.083333333 years old or maybe 83.0027 years old, etc. That is a continuous variable.

The reason for the distinction is that we must think of probabilities somewhat differently for the two types of variables. For a discrete random variable, we assign probabilities to the individual possible values. For a continuous random variable, we assign probabilities to intervals of values, not to individual values.

In Example 4, if we want to talk about the probability that a person’s age is reported as 12 years old, we need to find the probability of the age being in the whole interval of values of precise ages that we would report as 12 years old, which would be all the values between 12 and 13, including the value 12, and all numbers less than 13, but not equal to 13
