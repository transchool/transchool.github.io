5. Can man change or adjust Mars environment to make it more suitable for human habitation? How? Give five


Along with the sophistication of technology discovered by human civilization, trials to explore space and between planets are often carried out. One of them is inhabiting Mars which is close to Earth. Reporting from Sains.kompas.com, NASA said that humans are predicted to live on the red planet within the next 25 years.

At that time, humans were expected to be able to find the right technology and sufficient funding sources to set foot there. Unfortunately, this is still just an assumption. Given, the conditions and climate found on Mars is very much different from Earth. In fact, the planet may be uninhabitable and inhabited by human civilization.

Here are some reasons Mars is not habitable:

1. The existence of radiation that is deadly to the human body

in addition to mature technological preparation, the threat that comes from the planet Mars is radiation. As NASA's statement reported by Time, the presence of Solar Energetic Particles (SEPs) and Galactic Cosmic Rays (GCRs) in the Martian ecosystem are two of the most dangerous radiations for the human body. If you don't have advanced technology that can protect yourself from this, it's impossible for humans to survive on Mars.

2. Inadequate infrastructure and technology to adapt

There are many aspects to consider if you want to live on the planet Mars. Sources from tirto.id wrote that the availability of water sources, food, shelter and appropriate technology is the main thing that must be owned. NASA is reported to have found eight water sources on the red planet. However, there is no sophisticated and adequate infrastructure to process these liquids. Given, water is the main source of life for humans. Again, Mars is still uninhabitable for a while.

3. The environment and natural conditions of Mars are too extreme

Adapting quickly is key if you want to stay longer on Mars. In a journal entitled "Physiological and Psychological Aspects of Sending Humans to Mars" published in the Washington Academy of Sciences Journal, Paris wrote, humans must adapt immediately due to differences in geographical conditions and gravity between Earth and Mars.

Among them are the potential for vision damage due to environmental changes, bone and muscle strength will be reduced due to exposure to zero gravity, and the threat of cell damage due to the ionization process of cosmic radiation. Mars' environment is too extreme, it must be studied properly if it is determined to stay there.

4. A lonely planet that is uninhabitable

Although NASA often presents research that raises human hopes for going to Mars, this has been met with skepticism by some parties. Mars is a cold planet, full of deserts, little oxygen and very low gravity. The average temperature is -64º Fahrenheit or -53º Celsius, while the hottest is 80º Fahrenheit or 26º Celsius at the equator. Well, it's clear that the red planet may only be livable by advanced civilizations outside of humans.

5. Mental problems during the journey to Mars

Nola Taylor Redd, in a report entitled “How Long Does It Take to Get Mars?” The average distance between Earth and Mars is 225 million km. This of course requires a long and tedious journey for the crew of astronauts. It is feared, this can disturb the mentality of the explorers on their planes. A source from tirto.id wrote, Going to space can cause anxiety, depression, isolation, feelings of loneliness, and high pressure due to work.

Can humans alter Mars’ environment to make it more suitable for human habitation? How?

Jesus died - and then what really happened?

The first thing we would need to do is increase the planetary temperature. This could be accomplished with the construction of giant orbital mirrors focused on the poles. The focused sunlight would melt the trapped dry ice, thickening the atmosphere by several tens of thousands of pascals. The atmospheric pressure would likely be too low for humans to endure, but it would definitely be thick enough for liquid water to exist. With the thicker atmosphere and subsequent higher temperatures, the ice trapped at the poles would melt, likely covering the majority of the northern hemisphere in a vast ocean.

We could then introduce cyanobacteria to convert the atmospheric carbon dioxide into oxygen, and eventually nitrogen fixing organisms to convert ammonia into nitrogen. We would likely need to add additional nitrogen to the atmosphere, which could be accomplished with atmospheric chemical engineering. We could also mine nitrogen from the Venusian atmosphere. Venus only has 3.5% atmospheric nitrogen, but the atmosphere in so thick there is over right times as much nitrogen in the Venusian atmosphere as that of Earth.

This process will take centuries. Perhaps even millennia. However, after all this hard work, humanity could walk unaided on the surface of Mars. It would not be perfect. Mars is tectonically dead, so it has no significant global magnetic field. Colonists of Mars would be exposed to significantly more radiation on Mars as Earth, leading to a significantly higher occurrence of cancer and genetic disorders. The colonist would also need to carefully monitor the atmosphere, as the lack of a magnetic field would allow the solar winds to slowly strip it away. This process would be very slow, occurring over thousands of years, but it would require further efforts from atmospheric engineers. The lower gravity would also be a significant change. Someone born on Mars would have great difficulty adjusting to life on Earth, as they would have to adjust to being three times heavier than they once were.
