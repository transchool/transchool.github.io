How will you classify volcanoes with no record of eruption

Volcanoes are defined by scientists in a variety of ways, including size, shape, explosiveness, lava type, and tectonic movement. These different classifications sometimes overlap one another. A volcano with very effusive eruptions, for example, is unlikely to become a stratovolcano. One of the simplest ways to categorize volcanoes is based on their previous eruptive history and potential for eruption. Scientists use terminology such as "active," "dormant," and "extinct."

A dormant volcano is not active yet is likely to erupt again, while an inactive volcano has not erupted since the Holocene period. An active volcano that has erupted in the history.

Dormant Volcano:

Meanwhile, a dormant volcano is one that is capable of erupting and will most likely erupt again in the future, but has not erupted in a long time. Definitions get complex here as well, since it is impossible to differentiate between a volcano that is actually not active at the moment and one that would stay dormant forever. Volcanoes are often thought to be extinct as there are no written accounts of their activity. Volcanoes, on the other hand, can be dormant for lengthy stretches of time. Before their historic and destructive eruptions, the volcanoes of Yellowstone, Toba, and Vesuvius were all believed to be extinct.

Active Volcano:

There is currently no consensus among volcanologists on what constitutes "active." Volcanoes, like all natural features, can have very long lifespans ranging from months to millions of years. Many of Earth's volcanoes have erupted several times during the last several thousand years, but none actually display signs of imminent eruption. As a result, the term "active" should only refer to human lifespans, which are vastly different from volcanic lifespans. As a result, scientists often assume a volcano to be active only if it shows signs of unrest (such as unusual seismic activity or major new gas emissions) suggesting that it is about to erupt.

Extinct Volcano:

Extinct volcanoes are often referred to by geologists as volcanoes that have lost access to their magma source. Extinct volcanoes can be located all over the world, many of them are part of the Hawaiian-Emperor Seamount Chain in the Pacific Ocean or stand alone in some areas.
