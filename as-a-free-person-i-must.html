1. Calendar year: This refers to an accounting period that begins on January 1st and ends on December 31st of the same year. Many companies use the calendar year as their fiscal year to align with the tax year, making it easier to calculate their taxes and report their financial information.

2. Partnership: This refers to a type of business organization where two or more individuals share the ownership, responsibilities, and profits of a business. Partnerships can be formed with a written agreement between the partners that outlines the terms of the partnership.

3. Timeliness: This refers to the importance of financial information being available when needed. Timeliness is essential for making informed business decisions and for ensuring that stakeholders have access to accurate and relevant financial information.

4. Going concern: This refers to the assumption that a business will continue to operate indefinitely. When preparing financial statements, accountants assume that the business will continue to operate for the foreseeable future unless there is evidence to suggest otherwise.

5. Neutrality: This refers to the principle that financial reports should be free from bias and present information in an objective manner. Neutrality is essential for ensuring that financial reports are reliable and can be used for making informed decisions.

6. Manufacturing: This refers to the process of buying raw materials and converting them into finished products. In accounting, manufacturers must account for the cost of materials, labor, and overhead to determine the cost of the finished product.

7. Conservatism: This refers to the principle of being cautious when preparing financial statements by anticipating possible losses and expenses. Accountants should record losses when they are probable and avoid recording gains until they are certain.

8. Interim financial statements: These are financial reports that cover a period of less than one year. Interim financial statements are often prepared quarterly and provide stakeholders with up-to-date financial information.

9. Hybrid company: This refers to a business organization that engages in both the selling of services and merchandise. Hybrid companies often have a diverse revenue stream, making them less susceptible to economic downturns in any one industry.

10. Materiality: This refers to the concept that financial information should only be included in financial reports if it is significant enough to influence the decision-making of stakeholders. Accountants must use professional judgment to determine what information is material and should be included in financial reports.
