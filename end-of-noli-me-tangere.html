1. the ending of noli me tangere?​


This is the ending of "Noli Me Tangere" :

Basilio, who is still a youngster at the conclusion of Noli Me Tangere, is devastated by the death of his cherished mother, Sisa. At this point, a man who has been badly hurt approaches him. The man asks Basilio to construct a pyre out of firewood. After that, light it on fire while Sisa and him are on it. Basilio is gathering wood for the bonfire while the man murmurs:

“I die without seeing dawn’s light shining on my country…You, who will see it, welcome it for me…don’t forget those who fell during the nighttime.”

According to legend, Elias, a revolutionary leader of the Filipino people who was prepared to die for his nation, approached Basilio. Basilio decides to study medicine in El Filibusterismo. His intelligence is well renowned. Even though he initially hesitated, he ultimately decided to back Simoun's attempt to establish a revolution. Elias continued to exist in a way via him.

Jose Rizal, like the injured man, passed away before witnessing his nation's independence from the Spanish. He encourages us to embrace the light blazing on our nation—our independence, a future, and hope—through the quote. He also cautions us not to forget those who died at night. The list includes Jose Rizal, Marcelo H. del Pilar, and Andre Bonifacio. But, more than their names, we can learn from their lives.

The narrator provides updates on the lives of numerous individuals in the novel's epilogue. After Mara Clara enters the convent, Father Dámaso and Father Salv depart for Manila in the hopeless hope of being appointed bishop. Father Dámaso later receives an order from Father Salv to serve in a far-off province, and the news so upsets him that he passes away that night. Captain Tiago becomes an opium addict as a result of being extremely saddened by Maria Clara's decision to become a nun. Father Salv takes over as head priest of Mara Clara's convent after the ensign departs for Spain, leaving Doa Consolación behind. It is implied that he rapes Maria Clara, and despite her attempts to get assistance from the authorities, they ignore her—yet another example of how the ecclesiastical hierarchy is tainted.
