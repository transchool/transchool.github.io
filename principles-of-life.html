Self-Honesty

Admitting with Honesty that their deteriorating relationship with themselves, family, work or the community, is a result of their substance use disorder, is what spurs most people into getting help. Adhering to that same Principle of Honesty is what can help them to stay clean and sober later in recovery, when they are inevitably faced with the challenges and temptations confronted by all who lived with an addiction.

To those who have never been tortured by addiction, it may seem only too easy for a person to finally admit to themselves that they have a big problem with mood altering substances. It’s just a word, after all. But that word is true –maybe the first true word spoken in a very long time by the person whose brain chemistry was hijacked by casual, then chronic, substance use.

This is the critical first step in recovery. It is also the goal of Step One.

People trapped in active denial will almost never freely confess to having a problem. They will downplay the seriousness of their condition, minimize and conceal their destructive behaviors, and trivialize or deflect the concerns of others.

As long as this pattern of evasion and dishonesty is effective, the addicted person has almost no chance of successful recovery.

To be blunt, a person cannot and will not get the necessary help until they can plainly admit that a problem exists. That admission must be twofold.

The addict has to admit to himself that he needs help – that he simply cannot do it alone. Without that epiphany, no amount of begging or threatening will have any real impact.

Family, friends and co-workers become victims of Denial by minimizing the impact once the immediate crisis is over. They often threaten consequences for 2-5 years before those addicted to substances get help. They can be become Honest by setting limits and keeping their commitments to the threats. An intervention by family or friends is directed toward following through with consequences.

But as vital as first moment of self-honesty is, it’s not enough. Treatment for the entire family will keep the truth alive.

20. Explain the principle of sincerity. - example of the principle of sincerity? - How are you going to apply such a principle of sincerity in your student life? -


The principle of sincerity is a moral and ethical principle that requires an individual to be truthful, honest, and authentic in their actions and communications with others. This principle emphasizes the importance of being genuine and transparent in one's intentions and behaviors and avoiding deceit, hypocrisy, and manipulation.

One example of the principle of sincerity is when a person is honest with themselves and others about their feelings and opinions, even if it may be uncomfortable or unpopular. For instance, if someone is asked for feedback on a project or performance, they should provide honest and constructive criticism rather than sugarcoating their thoughts or holding back to avoid hurting someone's feelings.

As a student, applying the principle of sincerity can be beneficial in various ways. For instance, being honest with oneself about one's strengths and weaknesses can help set realistic goals and avoid over-committing to activities that may not be achievable. Similarly, being truthful and authentic in interactions with peers and teachers can help build trust and foster positive relationships, leading to a better academic and social experience.

To apply the principle of sincerity in student life, one can start by reflecting on their values and intentions and being mindful of how they communicate with others. One can also practice active listening and empathy, striving to understand others' perspectives and being open to constructive feedback. Additionally, one can seek out opportunities to engage in honest and authentic conversations, whether it be in the classroom, during extracurricular activities, or with friends and family.
