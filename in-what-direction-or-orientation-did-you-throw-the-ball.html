13. what force will you use to throw a ball?


The forces on a thrown ball after it leaves the thrower's hand are the force of gravity and possibly the force of air resistance. The force of gravity at the surface of the Earth is the gravitational acceleration at that location (9.8 meters per second per second) times the mass of the ball. The force of air resistance is proportional to the square of the ball's speed through the air, and its cross sectional area.
Although the force of air resistance is significant for most balls thrown with any reasonable speed, it is customary to neglect it in introductory physics because it is hard to calculate.

The force of gravity is directed toward the center of the Earth, but for thrown balls, the distance covered is small enough that the Earth is approximately flat over that distance, so the force of gravity is taken to be in a constant direction, downward.

These forces cause an acceleration of the ball in the direction of the total force, downward for the gravity part and backward for air resistance part. The acceleration is the magnitude of the force divided by the mass of the ball. This acceleration changes the initial speed and direction that the ball had at the instant of the throw, causing it to follow a curved path.

15. A ball player catches a ball 3.0 seconds after throwing it vertically upward. With what speed did he throw it, and what height did it reach?


Answer:

The ball player gave an initial speed of 14.7 m/s and the ball reached a maximum height of 11.025 m.

Explanation:

For a free falling body, the time throwing an object upward is the same time for the object to fall. It means that a 3.0 seconds catching the ball should be divided into two which yields to 1.5 seconds going up and 1.5 seconds going down.

For the formula, we use:

[tex]y=y_{0} +V_{0} t +\frac{1}{2} gt^{2}[/tex] equation 1

and   [tex]V_{1} =V_{0} +gt[/tex]   equation 2

where:

[tex]y[/tex]    is the vertical distance or height, where the object thrown

    upward has reached

[tex]V_{0}[/tex]    initial velocity, units in  m/s

[tex]V_{1}[/tex]    final velocity, units in m/s

[tex]g[/tex]      acceleration due to gravity equivalent to 9.8 [tex]\frac{m}{s^{2} }[/tex] (note:

       negative when the object goes downward and positive

       when the object goes upward)

[tex]t[/tex]      time, units in seconds

Information from the problem:

[tex]t=3.0sec[/tex]

[tex]g=9.8 \frac{m}{s^{2} }[/tex]

[tex]V_{0}=?[/tex]

[tex]y=?[/tex]

Solving the problem:

To solve for the initial velocity, use equation 2 then substitute the given information, we get:

[tex]V_{1} =V_{0} +gt[/tex]

[tex]0=V_{0} -(9.8)(1.5)[/tex]

Here, we use the final velocity, [tex]V_{1} =0[/tex] after the ball reached the maximum height. Use, negative sign for [tex]g[/tex] since the ball goes down.

[tex]V_{0} =(9.8)(1.5)[/tex]

[tex]V_{0} =14.7 m/s[/tex]

To solve for the height, use equation 1, we get:

[tex]y=y_{0} +V_{0} t +\frac{1}{2} gt^{2}[/tex]

[tex]y=0+(14.7)(1.5)-\frac{1}{2} (9.8)(1.5^{2} )[/tex]

[tex]y=11.025m[/tex]

Therefore, the initial velocity is [tex]V_{0} =14.7 m/s[/tex]   and the height the object reached is [tex]y=11.025m[/tex].
