1. 10 examples of culpable felonies and 10 dolo felonies


Culpable felonies are crimes or offenses that have been committed through the fault of one person. There is no intention to harm others but the harm was still done either through negligence, lack of skill, or even through the ignorance of the law. Culpa is the Latin word for fault or guilt. Some examples of culpable felonies are:

Accidentally running over a person with your vehiclePanhandling (asking for alms)Accidental death of people inside an elevator because building owner failed to have it repairedReckless imprudenceAccidental death or injury caused when firing your gun during New Year's EveDeath of a child due to not bringing them to the doctor in timeDeath of diners due to accidental poisoning when the cook failed to use proper and fresh ingredientsInjuring passengers when a driver swerved the vehicle to avoid an animal in the streetAccidental destruction of public or private propertyDeath of people in a fire due to a building's poor floor plan

Dolo felonies are crimes or offenses that are intentionally carried out to cause harm and injury to others. Ignorance of the law does not excuse someone if there is a clear intention to deceive the other party. Dolus or dolo means deceit in Latin. Some examples are:

TheftForgeryLibelHomicideEstafaGraftCorruptionRapeSexual assaultDomestic violence

7. how do you classify felonies and how are felonies classified​


Answer:

Some states use a “class” designation, such as A, B, C, and so on; others use “levels,” such as 1, 2, 3 and the like. Class A and level 1 felonies are the most serious, class B and level 2 are less so, and so on. States group their felonies in order to assign punishment on an orderly basis.


8. Explain why all felonies are crime, but not all crimes are felony​


A crime can either be classified as a misdemeanor or a felony, depending on how serious the offense is. Misdemeanors are less serious than felonies and carry lighter penalties. Felonies, on the other hand, come with at least a year (and sometimes decades or even a lifetime) in prison.

10. all felonies are crimes but not all crimes are felonies. Yes or No? Why?


Answer:

A felony is traditionally considered a crime of high seriousness, whereas a misdemeanor is regarded as less serious.[1] The term felony originated from English common law (from the French medieval word "félonie"), to describe an offense that resulted in the confiscation of a convicted person's land and goods, to which additional punishments including capital punishment could be added,[2] other crimes were called misdemeanors. Following conviction of a felony in a court of law, a person may be described as a convicted felon/felon. The centuries-old stigma of loss of wealth, status and speculated extreme gravity to the crime sees those personal descriptions widely deprecated, in favour of ex-convict or ex-criminal.
