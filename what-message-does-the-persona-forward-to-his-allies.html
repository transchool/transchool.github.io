“If We Must Die” is a Shakespearean sonnet written by the Jamaican poet Claude McKay in 1919. It is a poem of political resistance: it calls for oppressed people to resist their oppressors, violently and bravely—even if they die in the struggle. Though the poem has most often been read as a call to resist anti-black racism, it does not limit its call for resistance to a specific kind of oppression. As a result, it has served as an inspiration to a wide variety of oppressed people around the globe as they fight for their rights and freedom.

There are a lot of ways to interpret a poem. Here are the helpful steps for us to understand the meaning of every poem we read:

Analyze and figure out the maning
A poet does not directly express the literal meaning of the words he uses in every piece. He uses a variety of techniques to make the poem more colorful and unique. One of the ways is to use idiomatic expressions and figures of speech. These are often used to hide the real meaning of the poem.
Look for symbols.
Sometimes, a single line does not mean what we are thinking. We need to look for symbols and relate every line to get the real story behind the masterpiece. The poet's work can only be deciphered by deeply understanding the piece as a whole and not by stanza.
Look at the choice of words
Some poets like to use different elements to represent characters. Such as animals where the lion symbolizes sovereignty and power. Owl is used to symbolize wisdom and knowledge. The poet's choice of words can often lead us to understand the story.
Determine the storyline
A poem is not only written for fun. Most of the poems are written in relates to the poet's personal experience, historical background, or an image created from a dream or ambition. The storyline helps us relate to the persona's story and being in his shoes.
