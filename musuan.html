2. what province belong this volcano musuan​


Answer:

province of BUKIDNON

Explanation:

Musuan volcano, also known as Calayo, is an isolated, low grass-covered andesitic-to-dacitic lava dome and tuff cone that rises more than m above flat farmland in the province of Bukidnon in central Mindanao. Maso (1902) related that in a 1891 document a Jesuit priest had reported that a volcano along the River Palangui near a waterfall and where the river forms a very narrow canyon with steep high banks had "burst into eruption some four years ago and constantly emits vapors which burn everything about... there is no flow of lava, but it emits a column of smoke so sulphurous as to prevent any approach." Although Maso (1902) attributed this activity to Musuan, the geographic description does not match, so both the nature and location of this event is uncertain. PHIVOLCS at one time listed eruptions in 1866 and 1867, but had removed those eruptions as of 2020. A strong earthquake swarm occurred nearby in 1976.

Recent Eruption of Philippine Volcanoes

The most recent eruptions of the following Philippine volcanoes are:

Iraya – 1454Mayon – 2018Taal – 2020 to present (ongoing)Musuan – around 1886Bud Dajo – no known recent eruptions, but morphologically young

Iraya

Mount Iraya is located in the province of Batanes, and according to PHIVOLCS, it is classified as an active stratovolcano, having erupted in 1454 based on historical accounts and radiocarbon data.

Mayon

Mount Mayon is the most active volcano in the Philippines, having erupted more than 50 times since the 17th century. The most recent eruption in 2018 drew a lot of spectators hoping to see the famed river of lava that flows down the slopes of the perfectly-cone shaped volcano.

Taal

The most recent eruption of Taal took place in 2020, and two years later, small phreatomagmatic bursts are still occurring on the Main Crater. The eruption of Taal Volcano in 2020 displaced a lot of families who live in Batan gas.

Musuan

Musuan is a cinder cone, possibly born out of the nearby Kalatungan. The only recorded eruption dates back to 1886, and it was from a report written by a priest. This event, however, is uncertain as modern scientists are not sure whether the reported eruption really happened.

Bud Dajo

Bud Dajo is one of the cinder cones of the volcanic island of Jolo. It has no recorded historical eruptions, but the form of the volcano gives us a clue that it has erupted centuries ago.
