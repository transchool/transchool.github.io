Answer:

1328

Step-by-step explanation:

Use theory of conditional probability, we know that

P(X|Y)=P(X,Y)P(Y)

So, P(X,Y)=P(X|Y)P(Y)

Here let X1 be the event that the first ball drawn is white and let X2 be the event that second ball drawn is also white.

We want to find the joint probability P(X1,X2).

So, P(X1=W,X2=W)=P(X2=W|X1=W)P(X1=W)

P(X2=W|X1=W)=47

P(X1=W)=58.

So, P(X1,X2)=2056

Similarly now let Xi's represent events of both the balls being black.

Using the same calculation for black balls P(X1,X2)=656

So, the probability of both the balls have the same colour is

P(X1=W,X2=W)+P(X1=B,X2=B)=2056+656=1328

23. Four balls are drawn in succession without replacement from an urn containing 4 red balls, 3 blue balls, and 3 yellow balls. Random variable is K representing the # of blue balls.


Answer:

An urn contains 3 red and 4 white balls. Two balls are drawn in succession without replacement. If the second ball is​ white, what is the probability that the first ball was​ white?

I know several ways to solve this problem.

The first of them is using conditional probability.

What the question asks can be written as:

p = P(B1 = White | B2 = White)

P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)

So:

p = P(both white) / P(B2 = White)

P(both white) = (4/7) * (3/6) = (4/7)*(1/2) = 2/7

P(B2 = White) = 4/7

p = 1/2

Then: 50%

The second approach would be considering “symmetry”.

This one can be more difficult to understand.

It is based on the concept that getting B1 white given that B2 is white must be the same that getting B2 White given that B1 is white.

Why? This can be the difficult to understand part… Choosing one first, or calling one ball “the first” and the other “the second” in this problem would be the same as naming them in the other way around.

Also, knowing the value of the second ball first is like making it the first…

So, since the second case, the case where we change order is simpler, we calculate this second case, knowing that the probability must be the same.

If you already got B1 and is white, then there are 3 white balls and 3 red balls, so: p = 3/6 = 1/2

Or, in the exact naming in the question: knowing that the second is white leaves only 3 white balls remaining that could be the first and, also, reduces the total to 6… so it’s 3 over 6, that is, 1/2

The third approach would be a tree approach…

You can form a tree of cases:

Case W: B1 is White. (4/7)

Case W.w: from the cases where B1 is white (4/7 cases) only 3/6 of them have B2 also white … then P(W.w) = (4/7) * (1/2) = 2/7

Case B: B1 is Red (3/7)

Case B.w : from the cases where B1 is red (3/7), only 4/6 of them have B2 being white… then P(B.w) = (3/7) * (2/3) = 2/7

Now, how many cases have the second ball being White?

P(X.w) = P(W.w) + P(R.w) = 4/7

And how many of them have the first being also White?

P(W.w) = 2/7

So, from a total of 4/7 cases, only 2/7 have the first being white, and that’s one half of them… then the probability of the first being white in the cases where the second is white is 50% : p = 0.5 = 1/2

The fourth approach could be applying Bayes Theorem:

P(B1W | B2W) = P(B2W | B1W) * P(B1W) / P(B2W)

P(B2W | B1W) = 3/6 = 1/2

P(B1W) = 4/7

P(B2W) = 4/7

Then:

p = P(B1W | B2W) = 1/2

24. A box contains 2 red and 2 white, identical balls. two balls are drawn at random in succession without replacement. what is the number of elements in sample space for the experiment


Answer:

The sample space is the set of possible outcomes of a probabilistic “experiment”—in this case, drawing three balls from the box.

Unfortunately, we are missing three key details about the experiment: are the like-colored balls distinguishable, does the order matter, and is the draw with replacement? I will go with the standard assumptions of no for the last two, though you should be sure before you go on.

If the balls are indistinguishable, there are only four possible outcomes: rrr,rrb,rbb,bbb .

If the balls are distinguishable, though, suddenly there are (103)=120 possible outcomes! I will not write all of these down, but a typical element would be r2r5b4.

The advantage of considering the balls distinguishable is that then all outcomes are equally likely. If you are trying to compute a probability, that is really useful.
