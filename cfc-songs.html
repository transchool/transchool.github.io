13. what is CFCs and how do they affect the environment​


Answer:

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are organic compounds, and contain carbon, fluorine and chlorine atoms.

The most notable effect of CFCs on the environment is the depletion of the ozone layer. The ozone layer is essential to most life on Earth, as it shields lifeforms from a large portion of the UV (ultraviolet) rays from the sun. Depletion of the ozone layer can lead to various problems for humans and other lifeforms on Earth, which has lead to strong regulation being imposed on CFCs.

The good news (September 2010) is that UN scientists say that thanks to the 1987 Montreal Protocol (which phased out CFCs) the protective layer in the upper atmosphere has stopped thinning. They expect ozone will be restored to 1980 levels in 2045 to 2060.

27. The effect of humans adding cfcs to the ozone layer is to _____


Answer:

OZONE DESTRUCTION

Explanation:

a carbon-chlorine bond is being depleted when ultraviolet light waves (UV) strike CFC* (CFCl3) molecules in the upper atmosphere thus producing a chlorine (Cl) atom. The chlorine atom then reacts with an ozone (O3) molecule breaking it apart and so, destroying the ozone. A single CFC molecule can destroy 100,000 ozone molecules.CFCs do not easily react with other substances. In actuality, the sunlight causes them to deplete, which causes the division of  their molecules, causing the release of chlorine (Cl),and if the chlorine is released, it is able to react with ozone (O3), to form chlorine monoxide (ClO) and oxygen (O2).

Fortunately, chlorine has “natural enemies” as well, such as methane (CH4). Thanks to them, the natural ozone layer could recover over 50 years, as long as CFCs are no longer used on a global level.

The ozone depletion is also called as the “ozone hole”, due to the reality that the reduction of it is not uniform, but mainly concentrated in the Antarctic region, with reductions up to 70 per cent.
