Overview of multimodal literacy

A multimodal text conveys meaning th​​rough a combination of two or more modes, for example, a poster conveys meaning through a combination of written language, still image, and spatial design. Each mode has its own specific task and function (Kress, 2010, p. 28) in the meaning making process, and usually carries only a part of the message in a multimodal text. In a picture book, the print and the image both contribute to the overall telling of the story but do so in different ways.

Images may simply illustrate or expand on the written story, or can be used to tell different aspects of the story, even contradicting the written words (Guijarro and Sanz, 2009, p. 107).

Effective multimodal authors creatively integrate modes in various configurations to coherently convey the meaning required, ‘moving the emphasis backwards and forwards between the various modes' (Cope and Kalantzis, 2009. p. 423) throughout the text.

The complexity of the relationships between the various meaning or semiotic systems in a text increases proportionately with the number of modes involved. For example, a film text is a more complex multimodal text ​​than a poster as it dynamically combines the semiotic systems of moving image, audio, spoken language, written language, space, and gesture (acting) to convey meaning.

What are multimodal texts?

A text may be defined as multimodal when it combines two or more semiotic systems. There are five semiotic systems in total:

Linguistic: comprising aspects such as vocabulary, generic structure and the grammar of oral and written language

Visual: comprising aspects such as colour, vectors and viewpoint in still and moving images

Audio: comprising aspects such as volume, pitch and rhythm of music and sound effects

Gestural: comprising aspects such as movement, speed and stillness in facial expression and body language

Spatial: comprising aspects such as proximity, direction, position of layout and organisation of objects in space.

20. what are the semiotic systems and their meanings


Semiotics is the study of the process of making meaning from signs. Semiotic systems are- Sign, symbol and cue.

Basic Components of Semiotics

The basic components of semiotics consist of: sign, symbol, and cue. These three problems are included in the scope of semiotics because they allow communication between subjects and objects in the path of understanding as a basic component of semiotics. The explanation when the semiotic component is as follows:

a. Sign

Signs are part of the science of semiotics which marks a thing or situation to explain or inform the object to the subject. In this case the sign always refers to something real, for example, objects, events, writing, language, actions, events, and other forms of signs.

b. Symbol

The symbol is a thing or situation that leads the understanding of the subject to the object. The relationship between subject and object is tucked in the notion of inclusion. A symbol is always associated with signs that have been given cultural, situational, and conditional characteristics. Symbols are signs that have dynamic, special, subjective, figurative, and figurative meanings. In literary works, whether in the form of poetry, fictional stories or drama, there are various kinds of symbols, including: color symbols, object symbols, sound symbols, atmosphere symbols, tone symbols, and imaginative visualization symbols that arise from facial or typography.

c. Cue

A gesture is a thing or condition given by the subject to the object. In this state the subject always does something to inform the object that is signaled at that time. So, cues are always temporal (time). If it is suspended, the signal will turn into a sign or symbol. All three (signs, symbols and gestures) have nuances, namely very small differences regarding language, color and so on.
