27. A theory developed in 1912 by the geophycisist named Alfred Wegener about the changing surface of the Earth


Alfred Wegener developed the Continental Drift Theory. Even though this theory has long existed before him due to the beliefs of several geologists when they observed that the shape of continents on maps and globes may possibly fit together.

Wegener developed the theory further and published a paper about it while still giving credit to his predecessors who originally theorized this. He proposed that there was once a supercontinent, the Pangaea, which eventually broke apart and resulted in a continental drift. Wegener was also the first to invent or coin the term 'continental drift'. He presented evidence and hypotheses to support his theory and invented another term, Polflucht, which means a force produced by the earth's rotation. He also hypothesized that astronomical bodies or events may have also had a contribution to the continental drift.

Despite his efforts, the theory was not accepted during his time. This is because he was not a geologist, his calculations showed that the continents moved too fast, and his Polflucht hypothesis was not scientifically convincing.
